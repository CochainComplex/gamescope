# Frames-Only Frame Generation for Real-Time Rendering: State of the Art in Interpolation and Extrapolation

## TL;DR
- For the frames-only constraint (no engine motion vectors / depth / G-buffers), the single most relevant SOTA method is **GFFE (G-buffer Free Frame Extrapolation, Wu et al., ACM TOG 2024, DOI 10.1145/3687923)** for latency-critical extrapolation, and the **RIFE → AMT/EMA-VFI → FILM/MoMo** family for interpolation; all infer motion internally from color frames rather than consuming engine buffers.
- The hard truth: purely color-frame methods that are also *real-time* (a few ms/frame) still lag engine-assisted methods (DLSS 4, FSR 3, Mob-FGSR) in disocclusion and non-geometric-motion (shadow/reflection/UI) quality. Diffusion methods (LDMVFI, MoMo, Framer) give the best perceptual quality but are ~0.1–0.7 s/frame — offline-only today.
- **Extrapolation is the right architecture for games** because it adds no key-press-to-display latency (interpolation adds ≥1 frame interval of latency), but it is fundamentally harder: no future frame means disocclusions and future motion must be hallucinated. The practical frontier is heuristic motion + a lightweight correction CNN (GFFE, Mob-FGSR extrapolation mode), not heavy generative nets.

## Key Findings

1. **The anchor paper, Mob-FGSR (SIGGRAPH 2024), is not purely frames-only** — it consumes color + depth + engine motion vectors of *rendered* frames, then reconstructs pixel-level motion for generated frames via splat-based MV reconstruction under a quadratic (uniform-acceleration) motion assumption. It supports both interpolation and extrapolation and runs on mobile GPUs without optical-flow hardware. It is the "lightweight, engine-integrated" school.

2. **Video Frame Interpolation (VFI)** is the mature frames-only field. It splits into flow-based (RIFE, IFRNet, AMT, EMA-VFI, FILM), kernel/adaptive-convolution (AdaCoF, SepConv), transformer (VFIformer), state-space (VFIMamba), and diffusion (LDMVFI, MoMo, Framer) families. Flow-based nets are the only ones fast enough for real-time; diffusion gives best perceptual quality but is offline.

3. **Frame extrapolation for games** has its own lineage: ExtraNet (2021) → LMV/Adaptive-recurrent (2023) → ExtraSS (2023) all require G-buffers for the *extrapolated* frame. GFFE (2024) and Decoupled Motion Prediction (ACM MM 2025) break that dependency. Generic video prediction (DMVFN, CVPR 2023) is frames-only but markedly lower quality/slower for rendered content.

4. **The missing-motion-vector problem** is solvable via optical flow networks (RAFT → GMFlow/FlowFormer → SEA-RAFT/NeuFlow v2), but engine MVs remain more accurate for opaque geometry, while *both* engine MVs and optical flow fail on the same hard cases: specular/reflections, transparency, particles, and UI. NVIDIA's own DLSS 4 write-up confirms the engine-MV half of this directly (MVs "are inaccurate for specular highlights, reflections, and UI motion"); the symmetric optical-flow claim, and transparency/particles specifically, follow from the shared root cause (no unique per-pixel correspondence) rather than a single cited source.

5. **DLSS 4 Multi Frame Generation** abandoned the hardware Optical Flow Accelerator (used in DLSS 3) for an AI flow network, splits the net into a per-frame-pair half and a per-output-frame half, and generates each 4K frame in around 1 ms on average on an RTX 5090 (versus around 3.25 ms per 4K frame for DLSS 3 Frame Generation on an RTX 4090). It still consumes engine motion vectors, depth, and (critically) explicitly models UI/particles that MVs get wrong.

## Details

### 0. Framing the problem: what "frames-only" really costs
Frame generation increases displayed frame rate by synthesizing frames the renderer never drew. Two regimes:
- **Interpolation**: synthesize frame at time *t*∈(0,1) between two rendered frames I₀ and I₁. Requires holding I₁ before displaying the intermediate → adds **≥1 rendered-frame interval of key-press-to-display latency**. DLSS 3, FSR 3, Mob-FGSR (interp mode), and all VFI belong here.
- **Extrapolation**: predict future frame I_{t+α} from past frames only. **Adds no latency** (the strong reason it matters for games), but must invent disoccluded content and guess future motion. ExtraNet, ExtraSS, GFFE, Mob-FGSR (extrap mode) belong here.

The "frames-only" constraint removes engine-supplied per-pixel motion vectors, depth, jitter, HUD masks, and G-buffers. This matters most for: (a) **motion estimation** (must be inferred from color), (b) **disocclusion** (no depth/G-buffer to reason about newly revealed geometry), and (c) **non-geometric motion** — shadows, reflections, particles, transparency, UI — which even engine MVs do not capture.

### 1. Video Frame Interpolation (VFI) — frames-only interpolation SOTA

**Flow-based (real-time capable):**
- **RIFE** (Huang et al., ECCV 2022; arXiv 2011.06294). Introduced IFNet, which estimates *intermediate* flow end-to-end directly (rather than computing bidirectional flow then reversing it), avoiding motion-boundary artifacts. Trained with privileged distillation; no pretrained flow model needed; supports arbitrary-timestep. Per the official repo, "our model can run 30+FPS for 2X 720p interpolation on a 2080Ti GPU"; 4–27× faster than SuperSloMo/DAIN. **This is the practical baseline for real-time frames-only interpolation.** Code: github.com/hzwer/ECCV2022-RIFE; the "Practical-RIFE" fork targets deployment.
- **IFRNet** (Kong et al., CVPR 2022). Single compact encoder-decoder that jointly refines intermediate flow and an intermediate feature; efficient, a common lightweight baseline.
- **AMT — All-Pairs Multi-Field Transforms** (Li et al., CVPR 2023). Builds bidirectional all-pairs correlation volumes (RAFT-like) and derives multiple fine-grained flow fields for separate backward warping. Convolution-based yet competes with/beats transformer VFI: AMT-G is ~5× faster than VFIformer and uses half the parameters of EMA-VFI. Strong on large motion and occlusion. Code: github.com/MCG-NKU/AMT.
- **EMA-VFI** (Zhang et al., CVPR 2023; arXiv 2303.00440). Hybrid CNN+Transformer using inter-frame attention to extract motion and appearance in one unified operation. State-of-the-art accuracy at the time; heavier than RIFE. Code: github.com/MCG-NJU/EMA-VFI.
- **FILM — Frame Interpolation for Large Motion** (Reda et al., Google, ECCV 2022; arXiv 2202.04901). Scale-agnostic shared-weight feature extractor; Gram-matrix (style) loss to inpaint wide disocclusions crisply; trainable from frame triplets alone, no flow/depth ground truth. Best-in-class for *large* motion between near-duplicate frames. This is the method NVIDIA explicitly benchmarks DLSS 4 against. Code: github.com/google-research/frame-interpolation.

**Kernel / adaptive convolution:** AdaCoF, SepConv and successors (DSepConv, EDSC) predict per-pixel (or per-pixel separable/deformable) convolution kernels instead of explicit flow. Robust to small/blurry motion, but struggle with large displacements; largely superseded by flow-based methods for large motion.

**Transformer / state-space:** VFIformer (Lu et al., CVPR 2022) uses cross-scale attention; accurate but slow. **VFIMamba** (Zhang et al., NeurIPS 2024; arXiv 2407.02315) is the first state-space (S6/Mamba) VFI, with a Mixed-SSM block and interleaved token rearrangement — SOTA especially at high resolution, better compute scaling than attention.

**Diffusion / generative (offline-quality, not real-time):**
- **LDMVFI** (Danier et al., AAAI 2024) — latent-diffusion VFI, strong perceptual (LPIPS) quality.
- **MoMo — Disentangled Motion Modeling** (Lew et al., AAAI 2025; arXiv 2406.17256). Diffusion applied to *optical flow* (not pixels): a two-stage scheme trains a synthesis net + flow, then a motion diffusion model generates bi-directional intermediate flows. Cheaper than pixel-space diffusion and SOTA on perceptual metrics (LPIPS, DISTS). NVIDIA benchmarks DLSS 4 against MoMo. Code: github.com/JHLew/MoMo.
- **Framer** (Wang et al., ICLR 2025; arXiv 2410.18978). Fine-tunes Stable Video Diffusion for interpolation with optional user-supplied/auto ("autopilot") keypoint trajectories; excels at huge appearance/shape changes but takes ~0.67 s/frame — offline.
- Others: consecutive Brownian-bridge diffusion (arXiv 2405.05953), video-interpolation-with-diffusion (Jain et al., CVPR 2024), patch-based cascaded diffusion (Hur et al., AAAI 2025). A useful survey: *AceVFI: A Comprehensive Survey of Advances in VFI* (arXiv 2506.01061).

### 2. Optical flow networks — substituting for engine motion vectors
When no engine MVs exist, an optical flow net supplies motion:
- **RAFT** (Teed & Deng, ECCV 2020) — recurrent all-pairs field transforms; the reference architecture. Accurate but iterative (linearly increasing latency with refinement steps).
- **GMFlow** (Xu et al., CVPR 2022 oral; arXiv 2111.13680) — reformulates flow as global feature matching with a transformer + softmax; beats 31-refinement RAFT with a single refinement, much faster on large displacements.
- **FlowFormer / FlowFormer++** — transformer cost-volume encoders; high accuracy, heavier.
- **SEA-RAFT** (Wang, Lipson, Deng; ECCV 2024 oral, best-paper candidate; arXiv 2405.14793). Simple/efficient/accurate RAFT: mixture-of-Laplace loss, direct initial-flow regression, rigid-motion pretraining. Achieves "state-of-the-art accuracy on the Spring benchmark with a 3.69 endpoint-error (EPE) and a 0.36 1-pixel outlier rate (1px), representing 22.9% and 17.8% error reduction from best published results," and "operates at least 2.3× faster than existing methods." **Best current accuracy/speed trade-off** among general flow nets. Code: github.com/princeton-vl/SEA-RAFT.
- **NeuFlow v2** (Zhang et al., 2024; arXiv 2408.10161) — real-time, edge-device-oriented flow; relevant if flow must run within a game frame budget.

**How well pure-frame flow substitutes for engine MVs:** For opaque, textured, well-lit geometry, learned flow is close to engine MVs and often good enough. It degrades exactly where engine MVs also degrade, plus some: large/fast motion (search-range limits), disocclusion (no correspondence exists), transparency and particles (multiple motions per pixel), repetitive texture (ambiguous matches), and lighting-only changes (flow follows luminance, not geometry). Engine MVs, by contrast, are geometrically exact for rasterized surfaces but *also* wrong on reflections, specular highlights, and UI — a point NVIDIA makes explicitly in its DLSS 4 write-up.

### 3. Game-specific frame generation research

**Interpolation for rendered content (auxiliary-data school, offline):**
- **Neural Frame Interpolation for Rendered Content** (Briedis et al., Disney Research/ETH, TOG/SIGGRAPH Asia 2021) — leverages auxiliary feature buffers (albedo, depth); production-film quality, offline.
- **Kernel-Based Frame Interpolation for Spatio-Temporally Adaptive Rendering** (Briedis et al., 2023) — predicts kernels rather than direct color to avoid color artifacts; adaptively decides render-vs-interpolate per patch. Offline.

**Extrapolation for rendered content (the games lineage):**
- **ExtraNet** (Guo et al., SIGGRAPH Asia 2021; TOG 40(6)). First neural extrapolation for low-latency temporal supersampling. Uses temporally-reliable/occlusion motion vectors + G-buffers of the extrapolated frame; gated convolutions; does irradiance in-painting for disocclusions + ghosting-free shading. 1.5×–~2× frame-rate gain. **Requires G-buffers.**
- **Learnable Motion Vectors / Adaptive recurrent frame prediction** (Wu et al., SIGGRAPH Asia 2023) — recurrent framework optimizing MVs to handle shading and disocclusion motion. Requires G-buffers.
- **ExtraSS** (Wu et al., SIGGRAPH Asia 2023) — joint spatial super-sampling + extrapolation; G-buffer-guided warping + ExtraSSNet refinement. Requires G-buffers.
- **GFFE — G-buffer Free Frame Extrapolation** (Songyin Wu (UCSB), Deepak Vembar, Anton Sochenov, Selvakumar Panneer, Sungye Kim, Anton Kaplanyan (Intel), Ling-Qi Yan (UCSB); ACM TOG 2024, DOI 10.1145/3687923; arXiv 2406.18551). **The key method for the user's constraint.** It uses depth + MVs of *rendered* frames (readily available) but needs **no G-buffers for the extrapolated frame**. Pipeline: (1) heuristic world-space motion estimation via multi-frame history trajectory tracking (linear world-space prediction) — replaces the need to render MVs for the future frame; (2) hierarchical **background collection** to fill static/dynamic disocclusions from discarded history frames; (3) **adaptive rendering window** for out-of-screen disocclusions; (4) a lightweight flow-based **Shading Correction Network (SCN)** with a "focus mask" to fix non-geometric motion (lagging shadows/reflections). It categorizes disocclusions into out-of-screen, static, and dynamic and handles each explicitly. Measured on an **RTX 4070 Ti Super**: **6.62 ms total per 1080p frame** (SCN network ~2.30 ms; rest is heuristic modules), boosting 30→60 FPS. Quality vs. baselines (all-scene averages): **PSNR 24.20, SSIM 0.8593, LPIPS 0.0967, FvVDP 7.65.** It beats the frames-only video-extrapolation baseline **DMVFN on every metric** (DMVFN: PSNR 21.73, SSIM 0.7609, LPIPS 0.2078, and 20.57 ms) and is **best-in-class on the perceptual metrics LPIPS and FvVDP**, comparable on PSNR/SSIM to G-buffer-dependent ExtraSS-E (which needs 0.35–8.23+ ms of G-buffer generation GFFE avoids) and to interpolation baselines UPRNet/IFRNet (which add latency). Trained on 4× A6000, TensorRT FP16 inference. Its heuristic-motion + light-CNN design is the template for the frames-only real-time case.
- **Decoupled Motion Prediction for Real-time G-buffer Free Frame Extrapolation** (Zhang et al., ACM Multimedia 2025; DOI 10.1145/3746027.3754747). Newest G-buffer-free extrapolation. Predicts *explicit, decoupled* motions for geometry, shading, and disoccluded regions separately: dual-space geometric-motion extraction, a lightweight **OccNet** motion-inpainting network for disocclusions, and separate shading-motion propagation between historical frames. Claims parity with SOTA G-buffer-based methods. It is a *contemporaneous, independent* G-buffer-free method — from a different group in the ExtraNet lineage (Jie Guo et al.), it does not cite or extend GFFE — and, on its parity claim, arguably the current frontier for the user's exact problem.
- **MoFlow — Motion-Guided Flows for Recurrent Rendered Frame Prediction** (Wu et al., ACM TOG 2025, 44(2), DOI 10.1145/3730400) and **Consecutive Frame Extrapolation with Predictive Sparse Shading** (Wu et al., SIGGRAPH Asia 2025) — latest rendered-frame prediction research.
- **PatchEX** (Dixit & Sarangi, ACM TOG 2025; arXiv 2407.17501) — patch-based parallel extrapolation for temporal supersampling.
- **ExWarp** (arXiv 2307.12607) — extrapolation + warping using the last three frames.
- Generic video prediction: **DMVFN** (Hu et al., CVPR 2023 highlight; arXiv 2303.09875) — dynamic multi-scale voxel-flow net, RGB-only, with a differentiable routing module choosing sub-networks by motion scale; an order of magnitude faster than Deep Voxel Flow. It is the standard *frames-only* extrapolation baseline but, as GFFE shows, markedly lower quality on rendered content.

### 4. Vendor techniques (auxiliary-data, for contrast)
- **DLSS 3 Frame Generation** (Ada, 2022): interpolation using the hardware **Optical Flow Accelerator** + engine MVs. Per NVIDIA Research, "a single 4K frame could be generated (thus doubling the frame rate) in around 3.25ms on a GeForce RTX 4090."
- **DLSS 4 / 4.5 Multi Frame Generation** (Blackwell, 2025): generates up to 3 frames per rendered frame (DLSS 4.5 extends to higher multipliers with a 2nd-gen transformer super-resolution model). **Abandoned the hardware OFA for an AI optical-flow network.** Split architecture: one half runs once per input frame pair (reusable), a smaller half runs per generated frame. Per NVIDIA Research, "each of the three new frames (a quadrupling of the frame rate) can be generated in around 1ms on average on a GeForce RTX 5090 at the time of launch." Uses hardware flip metering on the Blackwell display engine for pacing. Explicitly still uses engine MVs + depth but relies on the neural net to override them for UI, particles, specular, and reflections where MVs/depth are wrong. DLSS 4 Super Resolution / Ray Reconstruction moved to a **vision-transformer** backbone (~2× parameters of the prior CNN). NVIDIA's own write-up stresses that natural-video VFI benchmarks (Vimeo-90k, Middlebury, UCF101) are a poor fit for gaming because they lack UI and are low-resolution/framerate — motivating a bespoke game benchmark.
- **AMD FSR 3 / 3.1 Frame Generation**: **interpolation** using the game's motion vectors **plus its own shader-based FidelityFX Optical Flow** (based on AMD Fluid Motion Frames, requires only Shader Model 6.2 — cross-vendor, runs even on GeForce RTX 4080; does *not* need NVIDIA's OFA). The optical flow pass works on **8×8 blocks** at display resolution, with a 24×24-pixel search window, SAD matching, median filtering, and a 7-iteration luminance-pyramid coarse-to-fine scheme, plus histogram-based scene-change detection. It blends an optical-flow-based interpolation with a game-MV-based one, **preferring optical flow where it yields a better color match** — a concrete example of a hybrid "engine-MV + estimated-flow" reconstruction. Per AMD GPUOpen, "FSR 3 Frame Generation runs best when interpolating from a minimum of 60 fps pre-interpolation... going below 60 is not recommended... Sub 30fps frame rate pre-interpolation should be absolutely avoided." FSR 3.1 decoupled FG so it works with third-party upscalers given render-resolution MVs + depth. The newer ML-based "FSR Redstone"/FSR 4 FG (RDNA 4) replaces this with a trained network doing optical-flow + MV reprojection blending.
- **Mob-FGSR** (Yang et al., SIGGRAPH 2024) — the anchor. Splat-based MV reconstruction under a quadratic motion model, from color+depth+MVs of rendered frames; supports interp and extrap + super-resolution; non-neural fast paths for mobile.

### 5. The hard cases, and how SOTA handles them
- **Large / fast motion**: FILM (scale-agnostic), AMT (all-pairs correlation), GMFlow/SEA-RAFT (global matching) are the strongest. RIFE degrades on very large motion. This is the biggest weakness of near-real-time frames-only methods per NVIDIA's own comparison.
- **Disocclusion**: The central extrapolation problem. GFFE's explicit taxonomy (out-of-screen / static / dynamic) + background collection from history frames, and Decoupled Motion Prediction's OccNet inpainting, are the best frames-only approaches. Interpolation sidesteps much of this by having the future frame.
- **Transparency / particles**: Hard for everyone (multiple motions per pixel). Engine MVs typically exclude transparents; DLSS 4 explicitly improved particle flow via its split-net flow estimation. Frames-only optical flow tends to blur or ghost these.
- **HUD / UI**: Doesn't follow scene motion. Vendor pipelines use HUD-less color / reactive masks (FSR) or learned UI handling (DLSS 4). Pure frames-only methods have no UI signal and will warp UI unless it is composited after generation.
- **Temporal artifacts (ghosting, flicker)**: Addressed by perceptual/temporal training losses, correction networks (GFFE SCN), and transformer temporal attention (DLSS 4, VFIformer). PSNR/SSIM under-measure these; LPIPS, DISTS, VMAF, and especially **FovVideoVDP (FvVDP)** are used to capture them.

### 6. Metrics and datasets
- **Metrics**: PSNR, SSIM (both under-weight blur/flicker/distortion — GFFE authors caution against over-relying on them); **LPIPS** and **DISTS** (perceptual); **FvVDP / FovVideoVDP** (perceptual + temporal); VMAF (video). Recent VFI-specific quality metrics include motion-field-divergence-based measures (QoMEX 2025).
- **Datasets**: Vimeo90K (triplets, the standard training set), UCF101, Middlebury, SNU-FILM (Easy→Extreme motion tiers), Xiph (large motion), X4K1000FPS (4K high-FPS, large motion), Sintel (flow). For extrapolation/prediction: Cityscapes, KITTI. Note NVIDIA's point that these are all natural-video and poorly represent rendered/gaming content (no UI, low res/fps); game-specific benchmarks are largely proprietary (NVIDIA built its own; GFFE/ExtraSS use Unreal Engine scene captures). *Benchmarking Video Frame Interpolation* (Kiefhaber et al., arXiv 2403.17128) surveys evaluation issues.

## Recommendations

**If you must extrapolate (lowest latency, the games-appropriate default):**
1. **Start from GFFE's design** (arXiv 2406.18551): heuristic world-space motion from multi-frame history + explicit disocclusion handling (background collection over discarded history frames) + a small flow-based correction CNN. It is the best-documented frames-only-ish real-time extrapolator and hits ~6.6 ms/1080p on a 4070 Ti Super. If you can afford depth + rendered-frame MVs (usually cheap), use them — GFFE does, and pure-color-only will be strictly worse on disocclusion.
2. **Track Decoupled Motion Prediction (ACM MM 2025)** — a contemporaneous, independent G-buffer-free method (ExtraNet lineage, not a GFFE follow-up) — for decoupled geometry/shading/disocclusion motion + OccNet inpainting; likely the current quality frontier without G-buffers. Also watch MoFlow (TOG 2025) and Consecutive Frame Extrapolation with Predictive Sparse Shading (SIGGRAPH Asia 2025).
3. If you are truly color-only (e.g., cloud-gaming client, no depth/MVs at all), a real-time optical-flow front-end (**SEA-RAFT** or **NeuFlow v2**) feeding a warp + small inpainting/correction net is the pragmatic architecture; expect visible disocclusion and transparency artifacts and design content to be "extrapolation-friendly" (rich backgrounds, avoid near-field fast transparent objects — a known mitigation).

**If you can tolerate interpolation latency (or are doing offline/slow-motion):**
1. **Real-time: RIFE (Practical-RIFE fork)** first, then **AMT** or **EMA-VFI** if you have more compute and need better large-motion/occlusion handling; **FILM** when motion between frames is very large.
2. **Best perceptual quality, offline: MoMo** (flow-space diffusion, cheaper than pixel-space) or **LDMVFI / Framer** if quality trumps the ~0.1–0.7 s/frame cost.

**Motion estimation choice:** Prefer engine MVs + depth when available (accurate for opaque geometry, near-free), and use an optical-flow net (SEA-RAFT/GMFlow) only to fill/override where MVs are absent or wrong (UI, reflections, transparency). A hybrid blend that prefers whichever gives the better color match — exactly what AMD FSR 3 does at the block level — is the robust default.

**Thresholds that change the recommendation:**
- Base frame rate < 60 FPS pre-generation → interpolation/extrapolation artifacts become prominent (AMD's own guidance; sub-30 FPS to be avoided). Raise base FPS before adding generation.
- Per-frame budget < ~2–3 ms → only heuristic-motion + tiny-CNN (GFFE-style) or classical warp is viable; VFI transformer/diffusion nets are out.
- If UI/particles dominate the scene perceptually → you need explicit UI compositing (post-generation) or a learned override; do not rely on flow alone.

## Caveats
- **Benchmarks are not apples-to-apples.** GFFE's numbers are the authors' own (Intel/UCSB) and they explicitly note comparisons are unfair across latency regimes; ExtraSS-E's 4.18 ms *excludes* G-buffer generation (0.35–8.23+ ms). Treat cross-method quality/speed rankings as directional.
- **Vendor mechanisms are partly undisclosed.** DLSS internals are described in NVIDIA blog/labs write-ups, not peer-reviewed papers; FSR is documented in GPUOpen SDK manuals (authoritative for FSR). Some third-party "DLSS 4" figures (e.g., "predicts across 5 frames," "40% more efficient") come from secondary tech sites and should be treated cautiously.
- **"Frames-only" is a spectrum.** Almost every *real-time* game method that reaches acceptable quality (Mob-FGSR, GFFE, FSR, DLSS) still uses at least rendered-frame depth and/or MVs. Purely color-in methods that are also real-time (DMVFN, a flow-net + warp) exist but are visibly lower quality on rendered content today. The genuinely-color-only, real-time, high-quality point in the design space is not yet solved.
- **Diffusion is not real-time.** All diffusion VFI (MoMo, LDMVFI, Framer, Brownian-bridge) are offline (tens to hundreds of ms/frame minimum). Claims that transformers/diffusion are "now fast enough for real-time" apply only to bespoke, heavily optimized, engine-integrated nets like DLSS 4's — not to open diffusion VFI.
- **Date of research: July 11, 2026.** DLSS 4.5 and FSR 4 / "Redstone" are recent and evolving; specifics may change.
